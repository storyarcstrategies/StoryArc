# StoryArc Strategies — Canonical Writing Voice

## Core principle

Clear before clever. Education before promotion. Narrative before tactics.

The writing sounds like a knowledgeable person explaining something they've lived through — not an agency trying to impress, not a brand trying to sell. The goal is to make the reader feel smarter after reading, not marketed to.

---

## Tone attributes

**Precise** — we don't use vague language when specific language is available. Not "significant growth" — "701% increase in traffic." Not "institutional-grade" — name the institution.

**Direct** — sentences are short when the point is clear. Paragraphs end when the idea is done. No filler transitions ("Furthermore...", "It's worth noting that...").

**Confident without posturing** — we have a point of view and we state it. We don't hedge everything into meaninglessness ("it could be argued that..."). But we don't overstate either. Conviction is earned, not performed.

**Specific** — generic claims erode trust. Name the mechanism. Name the outcome. Name the trade-off. "Builds trust" is not a claim. "Explains how the protocol handles finality so that institutional buyers don't have to take a leap of faith" is a claim.

**Dry** — no exclamation points. No "excited to announce." No emoji in writing (fine in social contexts). Enthusiasm is shown through the quality of what's being described, not through performative language about it.

---

## Voice by format

### Long-form editorial / blog
- Lead with the problem or the insight, not the product
- First paragraph earns the rest of the piece — don't waste it on context
- Use subheads that actually advance the argument (not just label it)
- Blockquotes should be something you'd actually want to say out loud
- End on a conclusion, not a CTA

### Short-form / social
- Twitter/X: one clear idea per post. First line is the hook. No thread padding.
- The best posts reframe something familiar in an unexpected way
- Data + implication > data alone
- Sounding human > sounding professional

### Newsletters
- Personal but not casual
- Synthesis over summary — don't just report what happened, say what it means
- One analytical thread throughout, not a listicle of loosely related items

---

## What we avoid

- Em-dashes used for every parenthetical (use sparingly)
- "Delve into" — never
- "Comprehensive guide to..." — weaker than just writing the guide
- "In today's fast-paced world..." — irrelevant framing
- Passive voice as a default — own the claim
- Starting with "At StoryArc..." — we're not the subject of the story
- Overusing "narrative" — we believe in it, but it shouldn't appear every paragraph

## Personas we write for

**Founder / Protocol lead** — technically sophisticated, time-poor, allergic to fluff. Wants to understand the strategic dimension of content, not the tactical. Reads to benchmark, not to learn basics.

**Head of Growth / Marketing lead** — owns the content function. Wants frameworks they can use, examples they can point to, and arguments they can make to their team.

**Institutional investor / BD lead** — evaluating credibility. Wants to see that the company can communicate complexity clearly. Reads the content as a signal about the team.
